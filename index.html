Importance of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is critical in our increasingly digital world. As new types of attacks are developed, cybersecurity needs to be updated continually through updating defensive hardware and software, making backup copies of important data, and instituting business recovery policies.

A defensive stance involves adopting an attitude and implementing strategies that make it challenging for attackers to compromise your digital life, safety, financial security, and privacy.

Effective cybersecurity means that data resides on personal computers attached to the network, and local security on all computers is important to foil attacks that breach the perimeter.

Challenges in Cybersecurity

  • Protecting personal computers is challenging
  • Many different types of attacks exist today, and attacks are constantly modified
  • New attacks are created regularly
  • No single defensive program exists - several different defenses must be in place
  • Specific technical skills are needed to counteract these attacks
  • Due to increased complexity of operating systems, unintentional vulnerabilities could be exploited by hackers

Operating System Updates

To address security issues, operating system vendors often put out updates through an automatic update service. These patches are software updates to address security issues.

Types of Attackers

Based on Motivation

Black Hat Hackers

Violate security for personal gain or to inflict malicious damage.

White Hat Hackers

Ethical attackers who probe systems (with permission) for weaknesses and privately provide that information back to the organization.

Gray Hat Hackers

Attempt to break into systems (without permission), not for personal gain, but publicly disclose the attack to shame the organization into taking action.

Based on Objectives

Cybercriminals

Attack for financial gain. Targets include individuals, enterprises, and governments.

Sample attack: steal credit card information

Script Kiddies

Lack knowledge to perform attacks on their own; use automated attack software. Responsible for approximately 40% of attacks requiring low or no skills.

Attack skill distribution: no skills (13%), low skills (28%), moderate skills (44%), high skills (15%)

Sample attack: erase data

Brokers

Sell their knowledge of vulnerabilities to other attackers or governments.

Sample attack: find vulnerability

Insiders

Organization's own employees/contractors/partners; cause 60% of data breaches through sabotage or theft of intellectual property.

Usually are moving to a new job and want to "take their work" with them.

Cyberterrorists

Ideologically motivated; almost impossible to predict when/where an attack may occur. Can be inactive for years.

May attack a small group that will affect a large number of people.

Hacktivists

Motivated by ideology; direct attacks at specific websites to promote a political agenda or retaliate for past events.

Many spread fake news and support conspiracy theories.

State Actors

Government-hired attackers with high skills and plenty of resources; most attacks directed towards foreign businesses to cause financial harm or reputation damage.

Unlike other categories, are guaranteed to keep working until success is achieved.

Are a cause of a new class of attacks called Advanced Persistent Threat (APT).

Common Attacks

Online Brute Force Attack

Continuous attempts to guess a password, largely mitigated by account lockouts after failed tries.

Zero-day Attacks

Attacks that use previously unknown vulnerabilities, giving zero days of advanced warning.

Password Spraying Attack

Uses common passwords across many accounts to avoid detection. Has a low success rate.

One or two common passwords are used trying to log in to several user accounts rather than multiple passwords to log in to one account.

Less likely to be logged out from too many failed attempts.

Password Collections

Attackers leverage stolen password databases to crack passwords efficiently. This is the most successful approach.

Social Engineering

Social engineering involves impersonation (identity fraud) and then playing out the role of that person on a victim. To appear genuine, the attacker must know something about the victim.

Example: An attacker could impersonate a help desk support technician and call the victim pretending there is a problem with the network. They would usually ask for the victim's username and password to reset the account.

Phishing

Most common attack based on social engineering - sending bogus email claiming to be from a legitimate business.

Users directed to an imposter website controlled by attacker, attempts to trick user into providing personal information.

Includes subtypes like "Smishing" (SMS-based) and "Vishing" (voice-based), as well as spear phishing, whaling, pharming, etc.

Example: an invoice scam sends a fictitious overdue invoice that demands immediate payment.

Typo Squatting

Redirecting users to malicious sites through typos in URLs.

Takes advantage of users who make a typing error when entering a URL address.

Attackers purchase domain names spelled similarly to actual sites.

Example: typing goggle.com (instead of google.com)

Hoaxes

Spreading false information to manipulate users into compromising their security.

False warnings, often contained in an email message claiming to come from the IT department.

Can be used as first step in attack. May warn of the virus and ask the user to take action, or even ask the user to call the attacker for help.

Social Networking Risks

The use of Internet-based social media platforms to stay connected with friends, family, or peers.

Personal data shared on social media can be used maliciously. The very nature of social networking carries significant security risks.

Background Media

Examples include phishing emails, typo squatting URLs, and social media hoaxes.

Malware Types

Malware is software that enters a computer system without the owner's knowledge or consent, performing unwanted and usually harmful actions. The number of instances of malware is staggering, as it continues to evolve to avoid detection.

Ransomware

Malicious software that "kidnaps" a user's computer and holds it "hostage" until a ransom is paid. Not easily traceable, as most cases use cryptocurrency. Top four targets: education, retail, business and professional services, government.

Blocker Ransomware

The earliest form of ransomware that manipulates the operating system to block all normal access to the system.

Typically contains a message pretending to be from a reputable third party agency.

Cryptomalware

A more malicious form that encrypts files (encrypting only some helps to evade detection).

The attacker demands payment to provide decryption keys. Often the key either increases in price over time or is unable to be purchased after a certain period.

A decryption tool is delivered in 99% of current cases. New variants can also encrypt files on connected devices/networks.

Eavesdrop Malware

Secretly collects data from its victims.

Keylogger

Captures and stores each keystroke a user types on the computer's keyboard. Can be software or hardware-based.

Captures passwords, credit card numbers, or personal information.

Software keyloggers can make screen captures or silently turn on the computer's web camera.

Hardware keyloggers are installed between the computer keyboard and the USB port.

Spyware

Tracking software deployed without the consent or control of the user.

Collects and distributes personal or sensitive information.

Technologies include:

  • Automatic download software
  • Passive tracking technologies
  • System modifying software
  • Tracking software

Masquerade Malware

Attempts to deceive the user and hide its true identity.

Potentially Unwanted Programs (PUPs)

A broad category that is often more annoying than malicious, such as advertisements displayed through pop-up windows.

Trojans

An executable that hides itself as performing a benign activity, but does something malicious.

Example: user downloads a useful program (like a calendar), yet this installation also installs malware that scans the system for personal information.

Remote Access Trojans (RATs)

A special type of Trojan that gives the threat agent unauthorized remote access to the victim's computer.

Attacker can change computer settings, browse and copy files, and use the computer to access other computers on the network.

Launchpad Malware

Infects a computer and uses it as a launchpad to send attacks to other computers.

Virus

Two types: file-based and fileless.

File-based: malicious code attached to a file. When launched, the virus unloads a payload and reproduces itself.

Fileless: takes advantage of native services and processes that are part of the operating system (LOLBins).

Worm

Malicious program that uses a computer network to replicate.

Enters a computer through the network by taking advantage of a vulnerability.

Modern worms can leave behind a payload and cause harm, such as deleting files or granting remote access.

Bot

Payload of malware that allows the infected computer to be remotely controlled to launch attacks.

The infected computer is known as a "bot" or a "zombie".

Botnets can be used for spamming, spreading malware, ad fraud, and mining cryptocurrencies.

Other Malware Types

Backdoor

Software code that gives access to a computer, program or service that bypasses normal security protections.

Logic Bomb

Computer code that lies dormant until triggered by a specific event, then performs malicious activities.

Rootkit

Malware that can hide its own presence, as well as the presence of other malware on the computer.

Malware Classification by Primary Action

Kidnap

Holds a system or data hostage for ransom (e.g., ransomware).

Eavesdrop

Secretly collects data from victims (e.g., keyloggers, spyware).

Masquerade

Deceives users by pretending to be something else (e.g., Trojans, PUPs).

Launchpad

Uses infected systems to attack other computers (e.g., viruses, worms, bots).

Sidestep

Bypasses security measures (e.g., backdoors, rootkits).

Defensive Measures

Defenses That Should Be Implemented

  • Managing patches
  • Running antimalware software
  • Examining firewalls
  • Stopping ransomware
  • Disabling unnecessary services

Antimalware Software

Software that can combat various malware attacks. Common types:

Antivirus

Scans a computer for infections, monitors activity, examines new files.

Works by matching to known virus "signatures". Due to many types of malware, is no longer considered a "magic bullet".

Comprehensive Antimalware Software

Provides intrusion prevention by analyzing information, reputation protection, classifying apps, and behavioral protection, blocking suspicious software.

Windows Defender

Anti-virus built-in for the Windows OS.

Patch Management

Managing patches is crucial for cybersecurity. Regularly updating software and operating systems helps protect against known vulnerabilities that attackers might exploit.

Firewalls

Software-based Personal Firewall

Designed to prevent malware from entering a computer, examines incoming data from the Internet or local network.

User can create an opening in the firewall just for an approved program to communicate across a local network or an Internet server.

Hardware-based Network Firewall

Designed to protect an entire network. Usually located at the "edge" of it as the first line of defense.

Intrusion Prevention System (IPS)

A system to stop attacks, analyzing the traffic in order to possibly spot patterns often used in the attack and cut it off.

Stopping Ransomware

  • Unplug devices when not used
  • Isolate the computer from storage devices
  • Create data backups, including continuous online backups
  • Maintain regular backups of important data
  • Use reputable security software with ransomware protection
  • Keep all software updated with the latest patches
  • Be cautious with email attachments and links
  • Use application whitelisting to prevent unauthorized programs from running

Password Security

When you create a password, it's converted into a scrambled form using a one-way hash algorithm. This hashed password is stored on servers. When you log in, the server hashes your entered password and compares it to the stored hash.

Guidelines for Strong Passwords:

  • Avoid dictionary words, phonetic words, repeated characters, and sequences
  • Do not use personal information such as birthdays, family member names, or addresses
  • Use longer passwords (length contributes more to security than complexity)
  • Incorporate non-keyboard characters

Surprising characteristics of weak passwords:

  • Password that can be memorized
  • Repeated use of the same password on multiple accounts

Password Managers

Technologies used for securing passwords are called password managers. There are different types of password managers, and they all provide a higher degree of password security than relying on human memory.

Multi-Factor Authentication

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) uses a secondary form of authentication, such as a code sent to your smartphone, in addition to your password.

General Defenses

  • Recognizing social engineering attacks
  • Taking steps to avoid identity theft
  • Reducing social networking risks
  • Staying informed about common cyber threats
  • Keeping your software updated
  • Disabling unnecessary services

Starting with personal security defenses is a logical and effective approach.

Mobile Security

Introduction

Mobile devices now account for over half of users' daily computing time, leading to phenomena like "nomophobia" (fear of being without a phone). This reliance makes wireless networks and the devices themselves prime targets for attackers seeking to capture unprotected signals and data.

Part 1: Attacks on Wireless Networks

Wireless networks are a critical vulnerability point. The main types are Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and Near Field Communication (NFC).

1. Wi-Fi

  • Operation: A Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) using radio frequency (RF) transmissions. The IEEE sets the standards (802.11 series), with versions evolving from 802.11 (1997) to Wi-Fi 6E (2020), offering increased speed and utilizing different frequency bands.
  • Equipment:
    • Chipset: Internal hardware for sending/receiving signals.
    • Wireless Router: The base station for home networks, acting as a Residential WLAN Gateway.
    • Access Point (AP): More sophisticated equipment used in businesses/schools, creating "cells" of coverage. Users "roam" between cells via a "handoff" process.
    • Mesh Network: A system with a main router and satellite "nodes" for extended coverage.
  • Risks & Attacks: Attackers on home Wi-Fi can steal data, read transmissions, inject malware, and download harmful content.

2. Bluetooth

  • Operation: A short-range wireless technology (up to 10 meters) used for pairing devices in various categories: Automobile (hands-free systems), Home Entertainment (headphones), Photographs (camera to printer), Computer Accessories (keyboards), and Sports/Medical devices (heart rate monitors).
  • Attacks:
    • Bluejacking: Sending unsolicited messages to Bluetooth devices.
    • Bluesnarfing: Unauthorized access to and theft of information from a Bluetooth device.

3. Near Field Communication (NFC)

  • Operation: A set of standards for communication between devices in very close proximity (~4 cm).
    • Passive NFC: Contains information that can be read but does not itself read data (e.g., smart poster).
    • Active NFC: Can both read information and transmit data (e.g., smartphone).
  • Uses: Contactless payment systems, entertainment tickets, office access, retail coupons, and transportation turnstiles.
  • Risks & Defenses:
    • Eavesdropping: Intercepting unencrypted communication. Defense: Be aware of surroundings.
    • Data Theft: Using a portable reader to "bump" and steal payment info. Defense: Turn off NFC in crowds.
    • Man-in-the-Middle Attack: Intercepting and forging communications. Defense: Configure devices in "send-only" or "receive-only" pairing mode.
    • Device Theft: Using a stolen phone for payments. Defense: Protect smartphones with strong passwords/PINs.

Part 2: Attacks on Mobile Devices

Mobile devices include Tablets, Smartphones, Wearables (e.g., smartwatches, fitness trackers), and Portable Computers (laptops, notebooks, 2-in-1s). They share core features like a small form factor, a mobile OS, wireless interfaces, apps, and data storage.

Mobile Device Risks:

  • Installing Unsecured Applications: Many apps lack security features.
    • Jailbreaking (Apple) / Rooting (Android): Circumventing built-in OS limitations to download from unofficial third-party app stores (sideloading). This bypasses built-in security protections.
  • Accessing Untrusted Content:
    • SMS/MMS/RCS: Text-based services (SMS), multimedia messages (MMS), and rich chat platforms (RCS) can contain malicious links.
    • QR Codes: Two-dimensional barcodes that can store malicious URLs.
  • Limited Physical Security: Devices are easily lost or stolen.
  • Constrained Updates: Security patches are delivered via Over-The-Air (OTA) updates. Support lifespans vary (Apple: up to 8 years; Google: up to 3 years), leaving older devices vulnerable.
  • Connecting to Public Networks: Risk of eavesdropping and connecting to an Evil Twin AP that mimics a legitimate network.
  • Location Tracking:
    • Geolocation: Identifying a device's geographical location.
    • GPS Tagging (Geotagging): Adding geographical data to media, increasing the risk of targeted physical attacks.

Part 3: Mobile Defenses

A. Wireless Network Security

  • Home Wi-Fi Security:
    • Secure the Router: Set a strong password (change from default), apply available security patches, and disable remote administration.
    • Enable Wi-Fi Protected Access Personal: Encrypts the signal and prevents unauthorized access.
      • WPA2 Personal: For all non-Wi-Fi 6E devices. Configure by enabling on the router and entering a passphrase on each device. Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS) can simplify this.
      • WPA3 Personal: For Wi-Fi 6E devices. Enabled at the router using the Wi-Fi Device Provisioning Protocol (DPP).
    • Other Settings: Change the SSID to an anonymous name and enable a separate guest network to isolate guest traffic from the main network.
  • Using Public Wi-Fi:
    • Watch for Evil Twin networks.
    • Limit activities to non-sensitive browsing; avoid banking or sending confidential data.
    • Use a Virtual Private Network (VPN) to create a secure connection over the public network.
  • Configuring Bluetooth: Disable it when not in use or set the device to "undiscoverable."

B. Mobile Device Security

  • Device Configuration:
    • Disable unused features that could serve as threat vectors.
    • Enable Lock Screen: Use a strong method (password, facial recognition, fingerprint) rather than a weak PIN (e.g., 1234, 1111).
    • Extended Lockout Period: Configuring the device to impose increasingly longer lockout periods after successive incorrect passcode entries.
    • Reset to Factory Settings: After a set number of failed passcode attempts, the device can be configured to erase all data.
  • Best Practices:
    • Do not jailbreak or root the device.
    • Do not sideload unapproved apps.
    • Back up device data regularly.
    • Use proper sanitization procedures before disposal.
    • Treat unsolicited text messages like phishing emails; do not call numbers contained within them.
  • Device Loss or Theft:
    • Prevention: Keep devices out of sight in high-risk areas, avoid distractions, use both hands, and be cautious on escalators or near train doors. Using wireless earbuds is safer than visible headphone cords.
    • Response Plan if Stolen:
      1. Call or send an alert via the carrier's app.
      2. Text the phone.
      3. Use the "find my phone" feature.
      4. Remotely erase data.
      5. Lock the phone and change associated passwords.
      6. Contact the mobile carrier.
      7. Alert the police.

Technical Information

  • MAC address - 48 bits, used locally, decimal form
  • Router - Gateway to Internet, connects different networks
  • Switch - Does not connect different networks, uses same network, switches based on MAC address
  • IP address - Used by router, not by the switch, decimal form, in blocks (4 bits) connected by dots
  • IPv4 - Old addressing system
  • IPv6 - New addressing system
  • HTTP - Used for communication between browser and website
  • HTTPS - More secure version of HTTP

To connect, a PC needs both a MAC address AND an IP address.

Checking IP Configuration

To check your IP configuration in Windows:

ipconfig

This command displays the current IP configuration for all network adapters on your system.

Miscellaneous

Identity Theft

Using stolen personal information for financial gain, leading to severe consequences for victims.

Security Strategy

Five key elements to creating a practical security strategy:

  1. Block attacks
  2. Update defenses
  3. Minimize losses
  4. Use layers
  5. Stay alert

Productivity Impact

Cleaning up after an attack diverts resources and reduces productivity.

Shared Responsibility

Cybersecurity is also a responsibility of users - knowledge of proper behavior is needed, as well as motivation to stay secure. There are practical steps everyone can take to enhance their security without resorting to extremes.
